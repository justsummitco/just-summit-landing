# Meeting Memory vs Meeting Transcripts

Transcripts are useful, but they are not the same as meeting memory. A transcript tells you what was said. Meeting memory helps you recover what mattered.

That difference is especially important for busy professionals who leave a call with a rough sense that something changed, but not enough detail to act confidently.

## A Transcript Can Become Another Inbox

A one-hour meeting can produce thousands of words. That is helpful for audit, quotes, or review, but it can also create another document you need to process.

If the output is too long, the work has only moved from the meeting to the transcript.

## Meeting Memory Is Structured

Useful meeting memory answers questions like:

- What changed?
- What decision was made?
- Who owns the next step?
- What detail did the client mention?
- What do I need to check before replying?

This is why summaries, action items, and searchable context matter.

## ADHD-Friendly Recall Needs Routes Back In

You may not remember the exact wording. You may remember the topic, person, project, or rough moment. A good recall system should support that.

Searchable meeting memory gives you more than raw text. It creates routes back into the conversation.

## Audio Adds Context

Tone, sequence, and surrounding discussion can matter. Sometimes the transcript line is less useful than the moment around it. A practical audio recall system should help you move between summary and source context when needed.

Just Summit is being designed around that broader recall problem: capture useful spoken moments, shape them into structured notes, and make them easier to find later.

## The Best Workflow Uses Both

Transcripts are good for completeness. Meeting memory is good for action. The strongest workflow uses transcription where useful, but does not force people to process every word again.

That is the difference between storing a meeting and being able to use it.
